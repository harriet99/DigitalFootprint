Design Iteration

This is an iteration with another design that I tried. In the initial stages of this project, I was drawn to the concept of a 'footprint' as the central theme for my data visualization, owing to its direct association with the word itself. I explored two primary designs that incorporated this idea. The first was to divide a single footprint into sections, each representing a different category of apps. The second concept was to create a series of footprints, each corresponding to a category, with the footprint size reflecting the number of apps in that category.
However, upon reflection, I found that while these designs were metaphorically relevant, they did not convey the usage intensity and personal engagement with each category in an intuitive manner. Moreover, they lacked the creative edge I was striving for. After further exploration, I decided on a fingerprint motif.

Final Design and Process

My project, titled "Digital Fingerprint: A Visualization of My Phone’s Apps," is a creative representation of the apps on my mobile phone, categorized into 10 distinct groups and visualized as a colorful fingerprint. This design approach was chosen to symbolize the unique and personal aspects of my digital footprint, akin to how a real fingerprint is distinct to an individual. The process involved first listing all 148 apps on my phone and then categorizing each app based on its primary function. The categories included Physical, Social, Travel, Financial, Food/Shopping, Entertainment/Other, School/Education, Calendar/Documentation, Utility, and Photo. Each category was assigned a specific color, and the cumulative layout of these categories formed the shape of a fingerprint. The fingerprint is made up of 148 lines. Each line represents one application, and as the lines with same color are gathered together, they form a layer of the fingerprint. This visualization journey from concept to final design involved several design decisions concerning color schemes, category divisions, and layout arrangements, ultimately leading to a final design that is both visually striking and meaningful.

In this visualization project, I integrated principles of data feminism by focusing on contextualization, narrative, challenging conventional norms, and inclusivity. Firstly, I went beyond basic categorization of apps by considering their roles in reflecting personal and societal dynamics, bringing a deeper context to the data. Secondly, I used the digital fingerprint design to narrate a story of my digital usage, moving past simple quantitative displays to a more nuanced and personal depiction. Thirdly, this project challenges traditional data visualization methods by using a unique, personalized symbol – the fingerprint. Lastly, the project was executed with an awareness of inclusivity, considering how my digital interactions might mirror wider societal patterns and access issues. This approach broadened the scope of the project, turning it into a reflective and socially-aware analysis of my digital footprint.

Here, I'm combining two sets of data that are not typically analyzed together: the names of the applications on your phone and the category of real-world activities they correspond to. Basically, I'm merging a personal, qualitative approach with a quantitative inventory.

Data Collection Process

It began with a comprehensive documentation of all the apps installed on my mobile phone, where each app’s name was carefully listed in an Excel sheet. This initial step was crucial, as it provided a clear overview of the digital tools at my disposal. The next phase involved a thoughtful categorization of these apps. I examined each app's primary function and utility to classify them into one of ten categories: Physical, Social, Travel, Financial, Food/Shopping, Entertainment/Other, School/Education, Calendar/Documentation, Utility, and Photo. This methodical approach was not just about grouping apps together; it was an exploration of my interaction patterns with these digital resources. By analyzing these categories, I gained valuable insights into which types of apps were most predominant in my daily life and how they collectively paint a picture of my digital habits and preferences. The data collection and categorization process laid a critical foundation for the visualization, ultimately reflecting my unique digital fingerprint through the apps I use.
